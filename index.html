Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saves a billion lives


Dr. Norman Borlaug, third from the left, trains biologists in Mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his life-long war on hunger

Here's a time line of Dr. Borlaug's life:

  • 1994 - Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933 - Leaves his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota, thanks to a Drepression era program known as the "National Youth Administration"
  • 1935 - Has to stop school and save up more money. Works in the Civilian Conservation Corps,helping starving Americans. "I saws how food changed them", he said. "All of this left scras on me"
  • 1937 - Finishes university and takes a job in the US Forestry service
  • 1938 - Marries wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Gets laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, he returns to school study under Stakamn, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack. but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, desinfectants, and other applied science.
  • 1942 - Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plants Pathology
  • 1944 - Rejects a 100% salary increase from Dupont, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to a head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties of each major climate on Earth
  • 1945 - Discorvers a way to grown wheat twice each season doubling wheat yields
  • 1953 - crosses a short sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeildling America breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. It goes on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat.
  • 1962 - Visit Delhi and brings his high-yieldling strains of wheat to hte Indian subcontinent in tile to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population
  • 1970 - Receives the Nobel Peace Prize.
  • 1983 - Helps seven African countries dramatically increase their maizeand sorghum yields.
  • 1984 - Becomes a distinguished professor at texa A&M University
  • 2005 - States "we will have to double hte world food supply by 2050." Argues that genetically modified crops are the only we can meet the demand, as we run out of arable land. Says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the bestbreeds."
  • 2009 - Dies at the age of 95.
